Brief Background and Project Description 
Background 
• Electronic Medical Record Keeping Systems (EMRKS) are designed to replace 
traditional paper charts with a faster, more accurate digital system. They help healthcare 
staff store and access patient information without dealing with handwritten notes, 
misplaced files, or slow manual processes. 
• These systems store all important patient information in one place, including medical 
history, prescriptions, lab results, diagnosis, and vaccinations.  
• Moving to a digital system also supports better decision‑making, since features like 
allergy alerts, test result warnings, and organized patient histories make it easier for 
providers to deliver safe, consistent care. Therefore, the shift to electronic records is 
essential for modern healthcare because it improves workflow, reduces errors, and gives 
both patients and providers quicker access to the information they need. 
Project Objectives 
• Provide secure, structured storage of patient medical records.  
o The system must maintain accurate, organized, and easily retrievable patient 
information. 
• Support multi‑user access with role‑based permissions.  
o Doctors, nurses, administrators, and patients each require different access levels. 
• Improve clinical workflows through automation and fast data retrieval.  
o Features such as automated logging, quick search, and structured forms reduce 
manual work. 
• Ensure compliance with healthcare data protection standards (e.g., HIPAA). 
o Sensitive medical information must be protected through encryption, 
authentication, and access control. 
• Enable seamless sharing of medical information among authorized professionals. 
o Providers should be able to access and share patient records securely to support 
coordinated care. 
 
 
Scope 
The scope of this project includes the core functionalities required to support a modern 
Electronic Medical Record (EMR) system. Specifically, the system will provide patient

records; appointment scheduling between patients and healthcare providers; prescription

Requirements Definition 
Functional Requirements 
Core functionalities: 
1. Patient registration and profile management 
The EMR system must support a complete patient registration workflow that allows new 
patients to create their accounts through kiosks, web browsers, or mobile devices. During 
registration, patients provide essential information such as their username, password, and 
personal details while the system automatically assigns the “Patient” role to ensure proper 
access control. The system must validate the uniqueness of usernames, enforce password 
complexity rules, and securely store all credentials using encryption. Once registered, 
patients can log in and manage their profile information, including updating their address, 
phone number, and email. However, SSN, date of birth, and PatientID must remain non-
editable to main data integrity. All updates made by the patient must be reflected in the 
Patient and Account_Information entities, ensuring that the system maintains accurate 
and current demographic data. 
 
2. Appointment scheduling and management 
The EMR system must support a comprehensive appointment scheduling workflow that 
allows patients to request appointments and doctors to manage them. Patients can select 
available dates and times, specify the reason for the visit, and submit appointment 
requests through the system. Doctors may approve, reschedule, or cancel appointments 
based on their availability. Appointment details including date, time, doctor, patient and 
reason are stored in the Appointment entity. This functionality ensures efficient 
scheduling, and provides patients with a clear overview of their upcoming and past 
appointments.

3. Secure sharing of records between authorized users 
Authorized medical professionals must be able to securely share patient records with 
other providers when necessary for coordinated care. The system must ensure that only 
users with appropriate permissions can initiate or receive shared records. All sharing 
events must be logged in the Access_Log entity to ensure time stamps if something were 
to happen with an unauthorized user. Therefore, this functionality supports collaborative 
care while maintaining strict security and privacy standards. 
 
4. Reporting and analytics on patient data 
The EMR system must generate a variety of reports for patients. This can include patients 
being able to access reports summarizing their medical history, prescriptions, 
appointments, and billing information. 
 
5. Record creation, modification, and retrieval 
The EMR system must support complete lifecycle management of all patient-related 
records, including their creation, modification, and retrieval. When authorized users such 
as doctors, nurses, or administrators create new records, the system must validate the 
input, assign the appropriate identifiers, and store the information in the corresponding 
entity within the database. Modification of existing records must be restricted based on 
user roles to ensure data integrity. For example, only medical staff may update diagnoses 
or treatment details, while patients may update only their demographic information. 
Every modification must be timestamped and associated with the user who performed the 
change to maintain accountability. Retrieval must be efficient and role-aware, allowing 
users to quickly access the records they are authorized to view. This functionality ensures 
that the EMR system maintains accurate, up to date information. 
 
6. User Authentication 
The system must provide a secure and reliable authentication process for all user roles, 
including Patients, Doctors, Nurses, and Administrators. Users log in by entering their 
username and password, which the system verifies against encrypted records stored in the 
User entity. If two‑factor authentication (2FA) is enabled for a user, the system must 
request an additional verification step before granting access. Upon successful 
authentication, the system must generate a new entry in the Acces_Log entity to record 
the login event, including the UserID, and action type. Failed login attempts must also be 
logged to support security monitoring. This authentication process ensures that only 
authorized individuals gain access to sensitive medical information.

7. Role-Based Access Control 
The EMR system must enforce strict role‑based access control to ensure that users only 
access information appropriate to their responsibilities. Patients may view only their own 
medical records, prescriptions, appointments, and billing information. Doctors and 
Nurses must be able to access patient records relevant to their care duties, including 
medical history, prescriptions, and test results. Administrators must have elevated 
privileges that allow them to manage user accounts, system configurations, and 
high‑level reporting features. The system must block any unauthorized access attempts 
and record them in the Access_Log entity. This role-based access control model ensures 
compliance with healthcare privacy regulations and protects sensitive patient data from 
improper access. 
 
8. Medical History Management 
The system must allow authorized healthcare staff to view and update patient medical 
histories. Medical history includes diagnoses, past conditions, allergies, and treatments, 
all stored within the Medical_History entity. Doctors and Nurses may update medical 
history depending on their assigned permissions, while patients may only view their 
history without making modifications. Updates to medical history must be associated 
with the user who performed the change to maintain accountability. This functionality 
ensures that medical records remain accurate, complete, and reflective of the patient’s 
current health status. 
 
9. Test Result Management 
The system must allow authorized staff to upload, update, and manage patient test results. 
Test results include the test name, result value, and date completed which are all stored in 
the Test_Result entity. Patients must be able to view their test results once they are 
released by the provider. This functionality ensures that patients receive timely updates 
about their health and that providers have access to accurate diagnostic information. 
 
10. Billing and Insurance Information 
The EMR system must allow patients to view their billing statements and insurance 
information. Billing records include the amount, date, and payment status, stored in the 
Billing entity. Insurance information (including provider name, policy number, and 
coverage details) is stored in the Insurance entity and linked to the patient. Patients must 
be able to review outstanding balances, past payments, and insurance claims through the

Non-Functional Requirements 
• Performance:  
The EMR system must deliver consistent and responsive performance to support the fast 
paced environment of clinical operations. This is because healthcare staff often access 
patient records during time‑sensitive situations, the system must process database queries, 
page loads, and record retrievals with minimal delay. Under normal operating conditions, 
the system should return results within clinically acceptable timeframes. It must also 
support concurrent access by multiple users including doctors, nurses, administrators, and 
patients without degradation in performance. Overall, the system must handle concurrent 
users without lag. 
 
•  Security:  
Security is a foundational requirement of the EMR system due to the sensitive nature of 
medical information. All data stored within the system must be encrypted both at rest and 
in transit to prevent unauthorized access or interception. User passwords must be hashed 
using industry‑standard algorithms, and two‑factor authentication must be available for 
all user roles to provide an additional layer of protection. The system must enforce strict 
access control policies based on user roles, ensuring that users can only view or modify 
information appropriate to their responsibilities. Unauthorized access attempts must be 
blocked and recorded in the Access_Log entity. Additionally, the system must maintain 
detailed audit trails of all user actions to support compliance with healthcare regulations 
such as HIPAA. These security measures ensure that patient data remains confidential, 
protected, and accessible only to authorized individuals. Overall, data must be encrypted 
and accessible only to authorized users. 
 
• Scalability:  
The EMR system must be designed with scalability in mind to accommodate the 
long‑term growth of patient records, user accounts, and stored medical data. As 
healthcare organizations expand, the system must be capable of handling increasing 
volumes of appointments, prescriptions, test results, and access logs without requiring 
major architectural changes. The database design should support horizontal or vertical 
scaling, allowing additional storage or processing power to be added as needed. The 
system architecture must also allow for future integration with external systems, such as 
laboratory information systems or insurance verification services. This ensures that the 
EMR system remains adaptable and capable of supporting evolving clinical and

administrative needs. In all, the system must accommodate growing patient records and 
users. 
 
 
 
•  Usability:  
It is essential for ensuring that both healthcare staff and patients can interact with the 
EMR system efficiently and without confusion. The system must provide intuitive, 
well‑organized interfaces that allow users to navigate easily between dashboards, patient 
profiles, appointments, and medical records. Forms must be clearly labeled, logically 
structured, and designed to minimize data entry errors. The system should also support 
accessibility standards to accommodate users with disabilities, ensuring that all 
individuals can interact with the system effectively. By prioritizing usability, the EMR 
system reduces training time, minimizes user frustration, and supports smooth clinical 
workflows. In all, there needs to be intuitive interfaces for both healthcare providers and 
administrative staff. 
 
• Compliance:  
The EMR system must comply with all relevant healthcare regulations, including HIPAA 
and other regional data protection laws. Compliance requires strict control over who can 
access patient information, how data is stored, and how long audit logs are retained. The 
system must enforce the minimum‑necessary access principle, ensuring that users only 
access the information required for their role. Audit logs must be preserved for legally 
mandated durations and must accurately record all access and modification events. By 
adhering to these compliance requirements, the EMR system protects patient privacy, 
reduces legal risk, and ensures that the organization meets industry standards for data 
protection.

User Authentication and Account Management 
The EMR system must allow patients to create accounts through kiosk, web, or mobile 
interfaces. During registration, the patient provides a username and password, while the 
system automatically assigns the “Patient” role and initializes the Two‑Factor 
Authentication (2FA) setting to false. The system validates that the username is unique 
and that the password meets security requirements before storing the encrypted 
credentials in the User table. 
Once registered, users authenticate by entering their username and password. The system 
verifies the credentials, checks whether 2FA is enabled, and if necessary, prompts for 
additional verification. Upon successful login, the system creates a new entry in the 
Access_Log entity to record the event.  
Security requirements include encrypted password comparison, session timeout after 
inactivity, and temporary account lockout after repeated failed login attempts. 
 
Patient Role Specifications 
Patients have access to a limited but essential set of system functions. They may view 
their profile information, including name, date of birth, masked SSN, address, phone 
number, insurance ID, and sex. This information is retrieved from the Patient entity. 
Patients may update only their address and phone number; sensitive fields such as SSN, 
PatientID, and InsuranceID are locked to preserve data integrity. Patients may also view 
their medical history, including past conditions, diagnoses, treatments, and allergies, 
which the system retrieves from the Medical_History entity. All access to medical records 
is logged in the Access_Log table. 
Patients may schedule appointments by selecting a doctor, date, time, and reason for the 
visit. The system validates that the selected time is in the future and that the doctor is not 


--- PAGE 10 ---
   
 
  9 
 
double‑booked before inserting the appointment into the Appointment entity. Patients 
may also view their prescriptions, test results, and billing information. Prescription data 
includes medication name, dosage, frequency, and duration, while test results include the 
completion date and result details. Billing information includes claim ID, amount, and 
payment status. All of these are retrieved through queries to their respective entities. 
 
Doctor Role Specifications 
Doctors have broader access privileges due to their clinical responsibilities. They may 
view patient demographic information, medical history, prescriptions, and test results. 
These different types of data are retrieved through joined queries across the Patient, 
Medical_History, Prescription, and Test_Result entities.  
 
Doctors may also create or update medical history entries, including diagnoses, 
treatments, past conditions, and allergies. These updates are written to the 
Medical_History entity and timestamped to maintain accurate clinical records. 
 
Doctors may prescribe medications by entering the medication name, dosage, frequency, 
and duration. The system validates that the referenced PatientID exists before inserting 
the prescription into the Prescription entity.  
 
Doctors may also manage their appointments by viewing their schedule and updating 
appointment statuses. These interactions ensure that doctors can efficiently manage 
patient care while maintaining accurate and secure records. 
 
Nurse Role Specifications 
Nurses have access to patient medical records similar to doctors but with more limited 
modification privileges. They may view medical history, prescriptions, and test results 
but cannot modify diagnoses unless explicitly authorized. Nurses may update minor 
medical notes or confirm test completion, with updates written to the Test_Result entity. 
These updates ensure that clinical workflows remain accurate and up to date while 
maintaining appropriate role boundaries. 
 
Administrator Role Specifications 
Administrators manage system‑level operations and have the broadest access privileges. 
They may create new patient records, insert account information, and associate insurance 
details. These operations involve inserting data into the Patient, Account_Info, and

accounts, enabling or disabling 2FA, and resetting passwords. They may review system 
access logs by querying the Access_Log entity to audit user activity and identify 
suspicious behavior. Administrators also manage billing operations by inserting new 
billing records or updating billing statuses in the Billing entity. 
 
 
System-Level Database Integrity Constraints 
 
Primary Keys 
The primary consists of: PatientID, DoctorID, NurseID, AdminID, AppointmentID, 
PrescriptionID, BillingID, ResultID. 
 
Foreign Keys 
• Appointment.DoctorID → DOCTOR.DoctorID 
• Prescription.PatientID → PATIENT.PatientID 
• Billing.PatientID → PATIENT.PatientID 
• Access_Log.UserID → USER.UserID 
 
Referential Integrity 
• Cannot delete Patient if related records exist. 
• Cannot insert Appointment with invalid DoctorID.
